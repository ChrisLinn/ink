There are two new papers on distributed RSA modulus generation. Both became online in the previous week. Let me briefly explain about the difference between them. As intro I will mention that the problem of how to generate RSA public key in a distributed fashion is 20+ years old and includes at least 10 different protocols. Some of them introduce exceptionally badass tricks. Mcgiver level. The latest work prior to the two from this week is the FLOP18 paper which includes one of these tricks - with the way they do OT (They also presented a clever way to make the protocol secure against malicious adversary).

Multiparty Generation of an RSA Modulus (https://eprint.iacr.org/2020/370.pdf) : This paper, written by some of my favourite authors, is introducing a new trick - instead of rejection sampling - they use CRT in a brilliant way. They keep using CRT throughout other parts of the protocol which makes for substantial improvement in efficiency over FLOP18. Another nice thing is that they use UC for the first time and it turns out to be very elegant and modular as it should be. For fans of the genre: along the way they deal with some subtleties in the simulators which makes the read even more fun. Highly recommended

Diogenes: Lightweight Scalable RSA Modulus Generation with a Dishonest Majority (https://eprint.iacr.org/2020/374.pdf): This highly anticipated paper, presents a full system, covering ALL the missing pieces to allow the protocol to run at scale. They use the CRT trick (its one of the things that once you see it, cannot be unseen), they replace the OT used in previous two papers with homomorphic encryption based approach, they claim to achieve identifiable abort, they use Ligero zero knowledge proof system for the malicious security (which means one basically put everything in a statement and prove it) and they introduce a new role of a Coordinator to help facilitate communication. They try to follow the line of UC security as well. This paper is crazy. Oh and It includes benchmarks of up to 4000 parties.

I agree with you to some extent.

Diogenes works under Universal Composabilit, which is by default *asynchronous*,
because adversaries can stall message delivery.


To solve the communication issue, they use an "untrusted" coordinator model.
They claim that it's untrusted but I agree with you that,
though the coordinator should learn nothing from the interactions, they still need to assume the coordinator will relay the messages, so it's not completely untrusted. It's not necessary to be your TTP. but more like semi-honest.

They also claim that the coordinator can be removed by being divided equally among all parties.
but
1) if doing it pairwise then it makes the communication complexity back to O(n^2) again.
2) if using HoneyBadgerBFT-like of BEAT-like broadcast, though it can lower then communication complexity (O(n) for HoneyBadgerBFT and constant for BEAT), it breaks their best-kind security story (n-1 active security with identifiable abort, i.e. dishonest majority) because of Liebig's law of the minimum.
